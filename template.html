Step 1 — Why

Problem & Goal

Internal tool, ~30-person software org. Linear = ticket/issue tracker, one ticket per problem. Core problem: no single source of truth — low-priority/small issues never get resolved, backlog grows unbounded and fragmented. Sharper pain: cross-functional misalignment — PM's view of the backlog and engineering's view drift apart; after any change, re-aligning priority across roles is manual and painful.

User Goal

Trust that the backlog reflects real, currently-aligned priority — without manually cross-checking PM vs. eng vs. design views.

Business Goal

Cut cross-functional alignment overhead — faster planning cycles, less time spent reconciling backlog views across roles.

  • Which role is our primary lens — PM, eng manager, IC engineer, or designer? (confirmed: all touch the backlog, but we need ONE to design for)
  • Is cross-functional alignment (PM↔eng sync) our chosen slice, or do we scope down to single-backlog hygiene only?

Discuss → Step 2: Who

  • Is this a "hair on fire" problem or a "nice to have"?
  • Who's paying? That changes the entire business model.
  • What's the wedge — which single pain point do you solve first?
Step 2 — Who

Users & Key Players

Primary User — Individual Engineer (IC)

  • Works day-to-day inside a ~30-person team's backlog — juggles many tickets across shifting priorities
  • Picks up tickets with no clear signal on which still matter now vs. gone stale/deprioritized
  • Files new issues as bugs/tech debt surface — unsure if a duplicate already exists
  • Wants to trust that their next pick is genuinely the highest-value use of their time — not guess or ask around

Key Players

  • Eng Manager / SDM — assigns ownership, chases stalled tickets, needs aggregate visibility
  • PM — owns the roadmap/priority source the backlog should stay aligned to
  • Leadership — stakeholder only (not in the day-to-day project) — wants occasional high-level rollup
Research: shadow engineers during ticket pickup — how do they currently decide what to work on next, and how often is their pick already stale/duplicate/deprioritized?

Discuss → Step 3: Context

  • Why this user over the other types? What makes them the best wedge?
  • How do they currently solve this? What would make them switch?
  • Are the key players enablers or blockers?

Step 5 — Ideation

Directions

Direction A — AI Priority Digest (chosen)

AI scans the full backlog + current roadmap/leadership goals daily, then surfaces a personalized "daily charge": what to pick up next and why it's the highest-priority, non-stale, non-duplicate option right now.

Pro: Directly answers the IC's core question — "what can I trust as my next pick?" — with visible reasoning, not just a list.  |  Con: Needs continuous roadmap ingestion + trust-building — cold-start risk if early priority calls are wrong.

Why chosen: directly serves our primary user (IC) at their actual decision moment — matches the Step 1 user goal of trusting the backlog reflects real, aligned priority.

Direction B — Blocker Escalation Assistant

When an engineer hits a blocker or discovers a task needs more resources/isn't feasible, AI helps them propose and escalate it — routes to the right person, frames the ask.

Pro: Solves a real, painful moment for the IC — removes friction in escalating blockers.  |  Con: Reactive, not proactive — only fires once the engineer is already stuck; doesn't address which ticket to trust before they start.

Why not chosen: solves a real pain point, but it's reactive — doesn't help the IC decide what to pick up in the first place, which is the core Step 1 problem.

Direction C — Vision-to-Breakdown Planner

For early-stage projects with a vision plan, AI breaks the vision into concrete tickets/milestones and proactively notifies stakeholders of blockers or plan changes.

Pro: Keeps big initiatives on track and visible — strong for long-horizon planning.  |  Con: PM/leadership-facing, not IC-facing — doesn't serve our chosen primary user's daily pickup decision.

Why not chosen: solves a different user's problem (PM/leadership), not our primary user (IC) — right idea, wrong persona for this session's scope.

Step 6 — Flow

Flow / Journey

①A AI-initiated: pushes today's top-priority task — the one this engineer "deserves to solve now"
or
①B Ad hoc / self-initiated: engineer has spare time (e.g. 4pm) — asks the AI directly "what's worth solving now?"
② Engineer reviews the task(s) + AI's reasoning — picks it up or defers
③ On pickup, status updates live — PM/manager/stakeholders see progress
④ Resolved — or if stuck/delayed, auto-escalates + notifies next owner
Edge cases: (1) ambiguous "completion" — partially solved gets flagged for review, not silently closed; (2) if owner needs another person's input, task reassigns and the new owner is notified — mirrors the real-world handoff pattern the team described.
